# common SA doc types

Pick the type that matches the job, then fill its skeleton. Every type leads with the point and ends with an action. One doc, one job.

## One-pager / brief

For a single decision or recommendation a busy reader must absorb fast.

- Lead: the recommendation or point, in two sentences.
- Why: the 3 reasons that matter, ranked.
- What it costs: effort, money, risk, in plain terms.
- The ask: the decision or next step you want.

## Q&A / FAQ

For answering a batch of client questions (often after a meeting or from a thread).

- Group questions by topic.
- Per question: a direct answer, key points, an optional example, a confidence note, and references.
- A "needs follow-up" section for what you could not confirm.

## Design / solution doc

For a proposed approach to a client problem.

- Problem and goal.
- Proposed approach, with the main alternatives and why they lost.
- Architecture or flow (link the diagram, do not inline a wall of text).
- Risks and mitigations.
- Path to production and next steps.

## How-to / runbook

For steps the client or team will follow.

- Prerequisites.
- Numbered steps, each independently checkable.
- Expected result per step.
- Troubleshooting for the common failures.

## Decision brief

For a fork the client or an exec must decide.

- The decision, framed neutrally.
- Options, each with trade-offs stated fairly.
- Your recommendation and why.
- What is reversible vs not.

## Structured questionnaire response

For procurement, security, compliance, technical assessment, or RFP-style questions.

- Preflight: audience, required format, sensitivity level, deadline, and whether approved source language is required.
- Batch summary: total questions, answered, partial, blocked, needs expert review.
- Group questions by domain and remove duplicates before drafting.
- Per question: direct answer, response status, rationale, evidence, caveats, and follow-up owner.
- Use approved language verbatim when policy, legal, or compliance risk is high; do not paraphrase sensitive claims.
- End with unanswered items and the exact review or approval needed.

## Executive narrative / meeting prep brief

For preparing a senior-stakeholder conversation, talk track, or follow-up narrative.

- Audience: who is in the room, what each person cares about, the language they use.
- Goal: the one decision, commitment, or next step the meeting should advance.
- Reframe: the commercial or technical insight that changes how the audience sees the problem. It must come from the audience's world, not from product doctrine.
- Narrative arc: Situation, Complication, Resolution, Ask.
- Stakeholder messages: one tailored message per key person.
- Objections: four to six likely objections, each with a response that acknowledges, reframes, and advances.
- Sharp questions: the questions to ask, not just the points to make.
- Delivery notes: what to keep short, what not to say, where to pause.

## Enablement / training plan

For recommending learning paths, workshops, or capability-building programs.

- Executive summary: why enablement matters now.
- Target personas: roles, current gaps, and why each is in scope.
- Recommended path: foundation, intermediate, advanced.
- Per item: title, audience, effort, modality, learning outcome, link (links supplied by the profile or the research step).
- Focus areas: map training to business goals without exposing confidential internal notes.
- Implementation: how to sequence flexibly without over-prescribing dates.
- Tracking: completion, adoption, knowledge-sharing.
- Next steps: who approves, who attends, how progress is reviewed.

## Account / partnership review

For QBRs, EBCs, renewal reviews, or strategic partnership updates.

- Cover: client, meeting type, date, owner, audience.
- Agenda: sections with rough timing and intent.
- Relationship summary: current state, major changes, what matters this period.
- Wins: three to six outcomes with quantified impact where possible.
- Health and usage: relevant adoption, value, or engagement trends with sources.
- Open issues: resolved vs open, status, owner, next step.
- Strategic alignment: client priority, challenge, proposed support, business outcome.
- Looking ahead: three to four priorities for the next period.
- Action plan: item, description, owner, status, date.

## Workflow showcase / reusable pattern

For documenting a successful workflow so others can repeat it.

- What was accomplished: task, deliverables, impact, time saved if defensible.
- Inputs: source materials, constraints, assumptions.
- Strategy: the key steps or decisions, with "why this worked" after each.
- Workflow architecture: a simple flow from inputs to processing to outputs.
- Capabilities used: capability categories, not vendor tool names unless the profile allows.
- Artifacts: links to outputs.
- Gotchas: errors, retries, limits, workarounds.
- When to use this pattern: the situations where it applies.

## Across all types

- Real headings, real bullets, embedded links. No bare URLs in body text.
- Inline citations for any claim a reader might challenge.
- Skimmable. Short paragraphs, a lead that states the point.
- Passes the `fe-deslop` skill. No filler, no banned phrases.
