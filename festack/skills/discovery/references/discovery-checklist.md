# discovery checklist

What a structured client picture needs before an engagement. Fill each from the profile-declared sources. Mark unknowns as unknown. The sources are whatever the profile names, never assumed.

## The pieces

1. **Who they are.** The organization, its business, its scale, where it makes money.
2. **Their stack.** The platforms and tools in use, especially anything you integrate with or replace.
3. **Incumbents.** What they use today for the problem you address, and how entrenched it is.
4. **Initiatives.** The current priorities, mandates, and funded projects that your work could attach to.
5. **Stakeholders.** Who decides, who influences, who blocks. Role, and what each cares about.
6. **Constraints.** Regulatory, security, budget, timeline, organizational reality.
7. **History.** Prior attempts, past vendors, what went well or badly before.
8. **Signals.** Recent moves (hiring, launches, reorgs, public statements) that hint at intent and timing.
9. **Solution-area map.** Break the client into the major workload, business, or technical areas relevant to the engagement. For each area: current state, incumbent approach, maturity, owner or champion, active initiatives, blockers, estimated opportunity, and confidence. "Solution area" is a generic lens; the taxonomy comes from the profile, not from any one product.
10. **Whitespace / penetration.** For each solution area, estimate current penetration versus plausible opportunity as a directional range, not a forecast. Use observed usage, active pipeline, stalled opportunities, adoption gaps, and benchmark comparisons when available. Mark assumptions explicitly.
11. **Opportunity lifecycle.** Group initiatives and use cases by stage: live or adopted, deploying, evaluating, scoping, early interest, paused or lost. For paused or lost items, capture the reason, its recency, whether it is still true, and a re-engagement path. Use generic stage names, not one company's sales-process labels.

## Bars

- Each piece is sourced and cited, or explicitly marked unknown.
- Stakeholders are named by role with what they care about, not a generic list. Champion and stakeholder assignments are evidence-graded: high confidence from direct ownership or repeated engagement, medium from partial linkage, low from title or role fit only, gap when no one is evidenced. Keep client-side stakeholders separate from your own team.
- Signals are dated and interpreted, not just listed.
- Confidence is stated per piece. A confident picture built on stale sources is a trap.
- The output tells a client-centered story: what is working, what is blocked, what is changing, what should happen next. Quantify where possible; keep executive views concise and push evidence detail into citations.

## Anti-patterns

- A research dump with no structure or interpretation.
- Treating a vendor marketing page as authoritative on the client's reality.
- Stopping at the org chart instead of finding who actually decides.
