# alignment checklist

The required pieces of an aligned engagement. Each must be present and sharp before `/scope-and-align` exits. Resolve observable pieces from the profile and context. Gate genuine ones. Do not accept vague answers.

## The pieces

1. **Vision.** The end state the client wants. The why behind the engagement. One or two sentences. Bar: a stranger could repeat it.
2. **Goal.** The specific objective of this engagement or deliverable. Narrower than the vision. Bar: it names what changes and for whom.
3. **Audience to win.** Who must be convinced, business and technical, named by role. Bar: you know who signs off and what each role needs to see.
4. **Requirements.** What the solution must do and must respect. Functional needs plus hard constraints (platform, data, regulatory, time, budget). Bar: constraints are explicit, not assumed.
5. **Success criteria.** How everyone agrees this worked. Bar: quantitative and measurable where possible, with units and a threshold. State the baseline and the target. "They were impressed" is not a criterion. "Triage time per claim drops below 5 minutes on the sample, from a 30 minute baseline" is. Reject vague criteria like "performs well" or "meets expectations".
6. **Context.** Current state, incumbents, history, prior attempts, political reality. Bar: you know what came before and what they compare you to.
7. **Risks.** What could sink this, technical and non-technical, with an early signal for each. Bar: named, not generic.
8. **Winning deliverable.** The artifact that wins (demo, doc, diagram, POC, or a sequence). Bar: chosen with one line on why it beats the alternatives.

## Socratic prompts to surface gaps

Use these to pull a vague answer into a sharp one. Ask the human only for genuine decisions. Resolve the rest yourself.

- Vision: "If this engagement fully succeeds, what is different for the client a quarter later?"
- Goal: "What is the one thing this deliverable must prove or produce?"
- Audience: "Who in the room can say no? What does that person need to believe?"
- Requirements: "What constraint, if we ignored it, would make this unusable for them?"
- Success: "What number or observation would let both sides agree it worked?"
- Context: "What have they already tried, and what are they comparing us to?"
- Risks: "What is the most likely way this falls apart, and what would warn us early?"
- Deliverable: "Why this artifact and not a cheaper one? What does it make possible?"

## Anti-patterns

- Accepting the stated ask as the goal without checking the underlying problem.
- Success criteria that cannot be checked.
- A deliverable chosen by habit rather than fit.
- Asking the human something the profile already answers.
